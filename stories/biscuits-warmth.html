A Story from Winchester
Biscuit's Warmth
Winchester, Tennessee  ·  January 1923

The morning Elias Cobb set out for the Pruitt farm, the sky was the particular shade of white that Winchester people knew better than to trust.

His mother had pressed a letter into his mittened hand at the door — a folded envelope with Mr. Silas Pruitt written on the front in her careful script — and told him to walk straight there and straight back and not to dawdle at the creek. Elias was seven years old and had made the walk to the Pruitt farm what felt like a thousand times. Down Elm Street, left at the old oak that had been struck by lightning twice and survived both, across the small wooden bridge over the creek, and up the gentle rise to the farmhouse with the green shutters.

He knew that walk the way he knew his own name.

He set off with his hands in his pockets and his chin tucked and the letter held carefully against his chest, and for the first stretch of it the morning was still and cold and ordinary. The snow started as nothing — a flurry, a suggestion, a few flakes wandering down from the white sky as though they hadn't quite committed to the idea yet. Elias watched them land on his coat sleeve and melt and thought nothing of it. He had walked through worse than a few flakes.

By the time he reached the creek bridge the flurry had thickened into something more purposeful, the flakes coming faster now and starting to stick, and the wind was picking up along the water in a way that made him walk faster. But the Pruitt farmhouse was visible from the bridge on a clear day — a comfortable shape on the rise above, green shutters, smoke from the chimney — and today he could still make it out through the white, and seeing it settled something in his chest.

Almost there. He'd been in worse than this.

He was twenty steps past the bridge, head down against the wind, when the storm changed its mind entirely.

It came down off the ridge the way the old-timers always said it did — not gradually, not with any warning a seven year old could have read — but all at once, like a hand slamming flat on a table. The temperature dropped so sharply Elias gasped. The snow that had been drifting became horizontal, driving into his face and eyes, and the wind that followed it was a sound he had never heard before, a tearing roar that seemed to come from everywhere at once.

He ran.

He ran with his head down and his arms up against the wind and his boots finding the rise of the hill more by memory than by sight, because the farmhouse had vanished entirely into the white. He ran toward where he knew it was, where it had always been, and when his hand hit the porch railing he grabbed it and held on and pulled himself up the steps and hammered on the front door with his fist.

The wind hit him from the side and nearly knocked him sideways off the porch. He grabbed the door frame with both hands and steadied himself and knocked again, harder.

Nothing. No footsteps. No light shifting under the door. No voice calling out.

He pressed his face against the front window, cupping his mittens around his eyes. The parlor inside was dim and still. Nobody home. Or nobody near enough to hear him over the storm.

He thought of Mrs. Pruitt — a round, warm woman who always had something on the stove and never minded a visitor, provided he left his muddy boots at the door. She wouldn't mind him coming to the back. She had told him so herself once, years ago, in the easy way of a woman who means it. You come to the back if the front's no answer, Elias. Just mind your boots.

He looked down at his boots. They were caked with snow and mud from the creek path.

He almost smiled despite himself and pushed off the porch into the storm.

The back of the farmhouse was worse — the wind funneled between the house and the old smokehouse in a narrow rush that hit him broadside and spun him half around. He found the back door by feel, both hands flat on the wood, and knocked until his knuckles ached.

Locked.

He stood with his forehead against the back door of the Pruitt farmhouse and felt the cold working into him in earnest now, past his coat and his mittens and down into the parts of him that had been warm when he left home twenty minutes ago. The letter was still pressed against his chest. He could feel it there, still dry, and for some reason that mattered to him though he couldn't have said why.

He lifted his head.

Through the white and the roaring he saw it — the barn, behind the house and to the left, its broad door swinging open on its hinges in the wind, banging against the frame in a slow irregular rhythm. Open. Unlocked. There.

And then, cutting through the storm as clearly as a bell, he heard it.

A neigh. Long and low and somehow certain, the way a large animal sounds when it knows something you don't.

Elias ran.

He hit the barn door at full speed, grabbed the frame, swung himself inside, and threw his weight against the door until the latch caught. The roar of the storm cut to a murmur. The wind became a sound instead of a force. The sudden stillness was so complete that Elias stood with his back against the door and his chest heaving and could hear nothing for a moment but his own breathing.

Then the warmth reached him. And the smell — hay and leather and something older, the particular smell of large animals that have lived somewhere long enough to become part of it.

And then something in the third stall moved.

Elias pressed himself back against the door.

✦   ✦   ✦

It was the sound first — a slow, shifting weight, the deep settled movement of something very large adjusting its position. Then the smell reached him, warm and grassy and animal. Then a head appeared over the stall gate.

Elias had seen Biscuit before. Everyone in Winchester had seen Biscuit. You saw him in the fields, or pulling equipment down the road, a distant enormous shape that you noted and moved past. You did not see him like this — up close, in a dim barn, his great dark head turning toward you with an attention that felt almost deliberate.

The horse's eye found him.

Elias stopped breathing.

He had not understood, from a distance, how large that eye was. Dark as creek water at night, rimmed with long pale lashes, and set in a face so broad and patient it seemed less like an animal's face than like something much older. The eye blinked once, slowly, the way a thing blinks when it has decided you are not a threat but reserves the right to keep watching.

Steam rose from Biscuit's back in slow curls where his heavy winter coat — a deep charcoal grey that Silas had buckled on him that morning — trapped the warmth of his body against the cold seeping through the barn walls. The steam rose and drifted in the thin light like something sacred, like the horse was breathing the cold air and returning it transformed.

Elias watched the steam rise and thought he had never seen anything so quietly alive.

He also thought that if that animal wanted to, it could step on him without noticing.

He stayed against the door.

✦   ✦   ✦

The cold found him again within minutes.

The barn was warmer than outside — anything was warmer than outside — but it was still January in Tennessee and Elias was seven years old and his coat was doing its best and losing. He could feel the cold working in through his collar, down through his sleeves, up from the frozen ground through the soles of his boots.

Biscuit had not moved. Had not looked away.

Elias looked at the stall. He looked at the horse. He looked at the stall again.

He took one step forward.

Biscuit's ears moved — not back, which Elias somehow knew was bad, but forward, tilting toward him with what seemed like simple attention.

Another step.

The steam was clearer now. He could feel the warmth radiating off the horse from six feet away, a gentle heat that reached for him like an invitation. On the top rail of the stall gate, draped with the casual permanence of something that had always lived there, was a blanket. Dark blue, worn to softness at the edges, smelling of horse and lanolin and a hundred cold mornings. Biscuit's blanket — you could tell by the way the horse's eyes moved to it and back to Elias, as if acknowledging that yes, that thing is mine, and yes, you may look at it.

Elias reached out and touched it.

The wool was dense and soft and warmer than anything had a right to be. He pulled it from the rail slowly, waiting for the horse to object. Biscuit watched him with that enormous patient eye and did not object.

Elias wrapped the blanket around his shoulders.

The warmth hit him so suddenly he nearly sat down right there in the aisle. He stood wrapped in Biscuit's dark blue blanket, two feet from the stall gate, and felt the cold begin to lose its argument with him.

He looked at the horse.

The horse looked at him.

"I'm not going to hurt you," Elias told him, though standing there wrapped in the horse's own blanket he understood this was a somewhat one-sided reassurance.

Biscuit breathed out slowly. The steam rose.

✦   ✦   ✦

The horse lowered his great head over the stall gate — slowly, with the deliberate patience of something that understands it must not move quickly around small frightened things — until his nose was level with Elias's face. The breath came out warm and grassy and enormous, fogging in the cold air between them.

Elias stood perfectly still.

The nose touched his cheek. Warm and velvet-soft and impossibly gentle for something attached to an animal that size. It stayed there for a moment, that great careful nose, and Elias felt something in his chest change, some tightly held thing releasing like a knot worked loose.

He reached up with one mitten and touched the broad flat plain of Biscuit's face.

The horse did not move.

✦   ✦   ✦

Elias was not sure whose idea it was to open the stall gate. He thought later that it might have been both of theirs at once — his hand finding the latch, and Biscuit stepping back to make room in a way that was clearly an invitation. The stall was large and clean, the straw deep and fresh. Elias stepped inside pulling the gate shut behind him and stood in the warmth that Biscuit's body had been generating all morning, a warmth so complete it felt almost like a room.

He put his back against the wall and slid down until he was sitting in the straw.

Biscuit turned to look at him. Considered him with that dark unhurried eye. Then — and this was the part Elias could never find the right words for, the part that came back to him for the rest of his life at unexpected moments — the horse folded. One great knee down, then the other, then that enormous body settling into the straw beside him with a long slow exhalation, a sound like a decision being made and accepted all at once.

The warmth was immediate and total. Like sitting beside a furnace that breathed.

Elias pulled the dark blue blanket tighter around himself and leaned into the curve of Biscuit's flank and felt the rise and fall of the horse's breathing become the steadiest thing in the world. Outside the storm screamed and threw itself against the barn walls. Inside there was only warmth and the smell of hay and the slow enormous heartbeat of the horse against his back.

He reached into his coat and checked the letter. Still there. Still dry.

He closed his eyes.

✦   ✦   ✦

Silas Pruitt had been out in it for forty minutes when he met Thomas Cobb coming the other way down the hill, lantern swinging, snow in his eyebrows, looking like a boy who had been sent to find his younger brother and was beginning to understand the seriousness of that errand.

They found the barn door unlatched.

Silas raised his lantern and stepped inside and stopped.

Thomas stepped in behind him and stopped too.

In the third stall, in the deep clean straw, Biscuit lay with his legs folded and his great head resting forward and his eyes open and calm, watching the door with the patient expression of an animal who has been expecting someone responsible to arrive for some time now. Curled against the curve of his flank, wrapped in a dark blue blanket, one small mittened hand resting on the horse's neck, was Elias Cobb. His cheeks were pink with warmth. His breathing was slow and even.

Silas took off his hat.

He stood in the doorway of his own barn in the January dark with snow melting off his coat and looked at what his horse had done and felt something move through him that he couldn't have named if someone had asked him to.

Biscuit had never lain down for anyone. Not in the twelve years Silas had owned him. Not for Silas himself, not for anyone.

Thomas started forward. Silas put a hand on his arm.

"Let him sleep a minute," Silas said quietly.

Thomas looked at him. Then at his brother. Then at the horse whose eye had not left the doorway since they'd walked in.

"Yes sir," Thomas said.

✦   ✦   ✦

The walk home was Thomas carrying Elias on his back, the boy half-asleep and still clutching the dark blue blanket which Silas had pressed into his arms without a word, and Silas walking alongside them with the lantern. The storm had begun to tire of itself. The snow was lighter now, the wind pulling back to a murmur.

Their mother was at the door before they reached the gate.

She said nothing for a long moment after Thomas explained. She looked at her youngest son, pink-cheeked and drowsy and wrapped in a horse's blanket, and she pressed her hand against his face to feel his warmth and then pulled him inside.

Later, when Elias was dry and fed and sitting by the fire, she came and sat beside him and asked him to tell her what it was like.

He thought about it for a long time.

"He just decided," Elias said finally. "He just looked at me and decided."

His mother looked at him. "Decided what?"

Elias considered this with the seriousness of a seven year old trying to be precise about something important.

"That I was all right," he said.

✦   ✦   ✦

Three days later his mother walked him back to the Pruitt farm herself and knocked on the door and spoke with Silas for a few minutes while Elias waited on the porch. When she came back she had her hands in her coat pockets and was looking at him with an expression he couldn't quite read.

"Mr. Pruitt says you can come visit Biscuit whenever you like," she said. "As long as you tell me where you're going first."

Elias looked up at her.

"And," she added, reaching into her coat pocket and producing the dark blue blanket, neatly folded, smelling of horse and warmth and that January night, "Mr. Pruitt says Biscuit would like you to keep this."

Elias took the blanket and held it against his chest the way he'd held the letter.

He kept it for the rest of his life.
