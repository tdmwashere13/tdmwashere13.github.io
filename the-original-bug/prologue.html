The Original Bug

Prologue

Sandy B. Patterson

At some point in the last few years, you started talking to a machine. Maybe it happened gradually — a search query that felt more like a question, an autocomplete suggestion that seemed oddly prescient, an app that appeared to know what you needed before you finished asking. Or maybe there was a specific moment: the first time you typed something into a chat interface and felt, unmistakably, that something on the other side was listening.

You are not imagining that feeling. Something has changed. The question this book asks is not whether artificial intelligence is real, impressive, or here to stay. It plainly is all three. The question is what it reveals about us — about the minds that built it, the habits that shaped it, and the assumptions we poured into it like concrete before it had a chance to set.

I want to be honest with you from the start about what this book is not. It is not a warning. It is not a celebration. It is not a technical manual, a policy brief, or a self-help guide for surviving the robot apocalypse. Those books exist, and some of them are quite good. This one is something different: an attempt to take seriously the possibility that our most urgent problem with AI is not the AI itself, but the human cognition underneath it — the shortcuts we take, the patterns we trust, the certainties we cling to long after the evidence has moved on.

I came to this subject sideways, the way most people come to the things that end up mattering most to them. I am not a computer scientist. I am not an engineer. I am someone who has spent a long time thinking carefully about how people think — how we reason, how we err, how we construct the stories we tell ourselves about the world, and how those stories tend to outlast the facts that inspired them. When AI started becoming something ordinary people talked about at dinner tables, I noticed something that nobody else seemed to be saying directly: that the machine was not introducing new cognitive problems into human life. It was inheriting the old ones. It was shaped by our data, trained on our language, optimized for our attention, and evaluated by our judgment. It was, in a very precise sense, built in our image.

That phrase is not an accusation. It is an observation, and a complicated one. We built something that reflects us — our curiosity, our creativity, our extraordinary capacity for abstraction and synthesis — but also our biases, our blind spots, our love of a confident answer, our impatience with nuance, our ancient and persistent tendency to confuse a plausible story with a true one. If we want to understand what AI gets wrong, we have to understand what we get wrong. And if we want to use AI wisely, we have to start using our own minds more honestly.

This book is organized around fifteen questions that I think matter. They are not the only questions that matter, but they are the ones that kept surfacing whenever I tried to think clearly about intelligence — human or artificial — and its limits. Each chapter takes one of those questions seriously, follows it into uncomfortable territory, and tries to come out the other side with something more useful than a tidy answer. Tidy answers are part of the problem.

I have tried to write this book the way I would want to read it: with enough rigor to respect your intelligence, enough story to hold your attention, and enough honesty to admit when the picture is genuinely unclear. The subject demands all three. Artificial intelligence is not simple, human cognition is not simple, and the relationship between them is not simple. But complicated things can still be examined carefully, and careful examination is, I believe, still the best tool we have.

So: you started talking to a machine. Let’s think about what that means.
